Week 1 · Grammar focus
Narrative Tenses
06 – 10 July
Mon 06/07 Narrative Tenses — Input day
GrammarReading
25 minGrammar: the 3 narrative tenses
Read the grammar explanation on British Council. Focus on: Simple Past (the main events), Past Continuous (background/interrupted actions), Past Perfect (what happened before). Write 5 example sentences from your own life.
🔗 British Council — Past Tense Review 🔗 Perfect English Grammar — Narrative Tenses
35 minFCE Reading Part 5
Do one full Part 5 practice text. Focus on finding evidence in the text for each answer — don't go by instinct alone. After finishing, read the whole text again without the questions and notice how the writer uses narrative tenses.
🔗 Cambridge — Reading activities 🔗 Flo-Joe FCE Reading
Today's self-check: Did you mix present and past while doing the reading exercises? Notice it — don't correct retroactively, just become aware.
Tue 07/07 Narrative Tenses — Practice
GrammarWriting
25 minGrammar: gap-fill exercises
Do 2 exercises specifically on narrative tenses. For each wrong answer, write the reason: was it a timeline issue? A background vs. event confusion? Irregular verb?
🔗 Exercise 1 — Perfect English Grammar 🔗 Exercise 2
35 minFCE Writing Part 1 — Essay
Write a full FCE essay (140–190 words). Topic: "Technology has made it harder for young people to focus. Do you agree?" Use the 4-paragraph structure: intro → point 1 → point 2 → conclusion. Don't edit as you go — write first, then revise once.
🔗 Cambridge sample essays + examiner comments
Writing challenge: Your essay is about the present, but your intro might want to reference the past ("Before smartphones existed…"). That's a great chance to use narrative tenses naturally.
Wed 08/07 Narrative Tenses — Listening
GrammarListening
20 minGrammar: narrative tenses in songs
Open the lyrics of All Too Well (10 Minute Version) by Taylor Swift. Go through the first 3 verses and identify every verb. Label each one: Simple Past / Past Continuous / Past Perfect. There are examples of all three.
40 minFCE Listening Parts 1 & 2
Do one full Listening Part 1 (multiple choice, short extracts) and one Part 2 (sentence completion). For Part 2, pay attention to word form — the answer is usually the exact word or phrase you hear, not a paraphrase.
🔗 Cambridge Listening activities 🔗 Exam English — FCE Listening
Thu 09/07 Narrative Tenses — Speaking
GrammarSpeaking
15 minGrammar: quick review
Without looking at any notes, write the 3 narrative tenses and one example sentence for each from memory. Then check. This is retrieval practice — struggling to remember is actually where the learning happens.
45 minFCE Speaking Parts 1 & 2 — Record yourself
Use your phone to record yourself. Part 1: answer these 3 questions out loud (30 sec each): "Tell me about a time you felt really proud of yourself." / "What was the last film you watched that you really enjoyed?" / "Describe a place that's important to you." Then do Part 2: describe and compare two photos from Google Images (search "people helping each other"). Speak for 1 minute. Listen back and count: how many tense errors do you notice yourself?
Speaking tip: All 3 Part 1 questions above naturally require past tenses. That's deliberate — use them to practise narrative tenses in a speaking context.
Fri 10/07 Narrative Tenses — Full review day
GrammarUse of English
20 minGrammar: error hunt
Go back to the essay you wrote on Tuesday. Read it out loud. Find and correct any tense errors. Then rewrite any sentence where you used Simple Past when Past Perfect would be more accurate (i.e. something that happened before another past event).
40 minFCE Use of English Parts 1 & 2
Do one Part 1 (multiple choice cloze) and one Part 2 (open cloze). These test vocabulary and grammar in context together. For each wrong answer, identify whether it was a vocabulary gap or a grammar issue.
🔗 Flo-Joe — Use of English 🔗 Exam English — Part 1
End of week 1: You've now seen narrative tenses in reading, writing, listening, speaking, and use of english. That's the whole exam in one week.

Week 2 · Grammar focus
Conditionals (2 & 3)
13 – 17 July
Mon 13/07 Conditionals — Input day
GrammarReading
25 minGrammar: Conditionals 2 & 3
Read the explanation, then write 4 sentences of your own: 2 with conditional 2 (hypothetical present/future: If I were…, I would…) and 2 with conditional 3 (hypothetical past: If I had…, I would have…). Make them about real things — your course, your relationship, Narnia, Hunger Games, faith.
🔗 British Council — Conditional 2 🔗 British Council — Conditional 3
35 minFCE Reading Part 6 — Gapped text
This is the hardest Reading part. You remove 6 sentences from a text and choose where each one goes back. Focus on linking devices (however, therefore, moreover) and pronoun reference (he, she, it, they, this) to find the right position.
🔗 Flo-Joe — Reading Part 6
Tue 14/07 Conditionals — Hunger Games
GrammarSpeaking
25 minGrammar: conditionals in storytelling
Think about The Hunger Games. Write answers to these prompts using conditional 3: "If Katniss hadn't volunteered, what would have happened to Prim?" / "If Peeta hadn't helped Katniss in the forest, what would she have done?" / "If the Capitol hadn't existed, how would Panem have been different?" Aim for 2–3 sentences per answer.
35 minFCE Speaking Part 3 & 4 practice
Part 3 is a collaborative task (usually done with a partner). Alone, talk through this task out loud: You're deciding which activities would help young people develop important life skills — discuss: learning to cook / doing voluntary work / playing team sports / learning a musical instrument / travelling abroad. Then answer Part 4 questions: "Do you think schools do enough to prepare young people for adult life?" Record yourself.
Connection: Conditional 3 is perfect for discussing Hunger Games, Narnia, or any story where things could have gone differently. It also appears constantly in FCE Writing and Speaking.
Wed 15/07 Conditionals — Writing
GrammarWriting
20 minGrammar: mixed conditionals
A mixed conditional combines a past condition with a present result: "If I had studied harder last year, I would be at C1 now." Write 3 mixed conditional sentences that are genuinely true for you. These are harder — take your time.
🔗 Mixed Conditionals — Perfect English Grammar
40 minFCE Writing Part 2 — Article
Write a magazine article (140–190 words) for a student magazine. Title: "What would the world look like without social media?" Use at least 2 conditionals naturally in your article. Think about: engaging opening, two or three ideas, conclusion that leaves the reader thinking.
Thu 16/07 Conditionals — Use of English
GrammarUse of English
20 minGrammar: key word transformation with conditionals
This is Use of English Part 4. The key words for conditionals are often: unless, provided, as long as, had, were, if only. Practice: rewrite these sentences keeping the meaning: "You will only pass the exam if you study." → key word: UNLESS / "I regret not going to bed earlier." → key word: HAD
🔗 Flo-Joe — Key Word Transformations
40 minFCE Use of English Parts 3 & 4
Part 3 is word formation (adding prefixes/suffixes). Part 4 is key word transformation (6 sentences, rewrite using the key word, 2–5 words). These two parts together are worth a lot — worth practising them every week.
🔗 Part 3 — Word Formation 🔗 Part 4 — Key Word Transformation
Fri 17/07 Conditionals — Free day
GrammarListening
20 minGrammar: in music
Find a Taylor Swift song that uses conditionals. Good options: The 1 ("If one thing had been different…"), Back to December, Last Kiss. Write out the conditional sentences you find and label them: conditional 2, 3, or mixed.
40 minFCE Listening Parts 3 & 4
Part 3 is multiple matching (5 speakers, 8 options). Part 4 is multiple choice (7 questions, longer extract). Listen twice each time. Don't just match the first word you hear — the correct answer usually paraphrases what the speaker says.
🔗 Exam English — FCE Listening
End of week 2: Conditionals 2 and 3 are some of the most common structures in FCE Writing and Speaking. If they feel natural now, that's a big advantage going into the exam.

Week 3 · Grammar focus
Modal Verbs & Speculation
20 – 24 July
Mon 20/07 Modals — Input day
GrammarReading
25 minGrammar: modals for speculation
Focus on these 6 modal uses: must be (strong deduction), can't be (strong negative deduction), might/could be (possibility), must have been (past deduction), can't have been (past negative deduction), might have been (past possibility). Write one sentence for each using people or situations you know.
🔗 British Council — Modals for Deduction
35 minFCE Reading Part 7 — Multiple matching
Part 7 has 10 questions matched to 4–5 short texts. Read all texts first, then go through the questions. Key skill: recognise paraphrasing — the question rarely uses the same words as the text.
🔗 Flo-Joe — Reading Part 7
Tue 21/07 Modals — FCE Speaking Part 2
GrammarSpeaking
20 minGrammar: modals in photo descriptions
FCE Speaking Part 2 requires speculation about photos. Practise these phrases out loud: "They might be…" / "She could be feeling…" / "It looks as if…" / "I imagine they must be…" / "He can't be very happy because…". Say each one out loud 3 times, then use them in a sentence about a real situation.
40 minFCE Speaking Part 2 — 4 rounds
Go to Google Images and search these 4 pairs, one at a time. Describe each pair for 1 minute, then answer the short follow-up question. Record yourself each time.

1. Search: "people celebrating outdoors" → Why might these people be celebrating?
2. Search: "students studying together" → Which situation looks more productive?
3. Search: "person alone in city" → How might this person be feeling?
4. Search: "technology in everyday life" → What do these images say about modern life?
Key insight: Modals are the grammar of Speaking Part 2. Every time you speculate about a photo, you're using them. The examiner is listening for variety — don't just use "might" for everything.
Wed 22/07 Modals — Writing
GrammarWriting
20 minGrammar: modals in essays
Modals make essays more nuanced and less absolute. Compare: "Technology is bad for relationships" vs. "Technology might damage relationships if used excessively." Rewrite these sentences to make them more nuanced using modals: "AI will replace all jobs." / "Social media causes depression." / "Young people don't read anymore."
40 minFCE Writing Part 2 — Email
Write a semi-formal email (140–190 words). Situation: You've seen an advertisement for a summer language course abroad. Write an email to the school asking for more information about the course, accommodation and costs. Focus on register: polite but not too formal. Use could you, would it be possible, I'd like to know.
🔗 Cambridge sample answers
Thu 23/07 Modals — Listening deep dive
GrammarListening
20 minGrammar: modals + past — error correction
Find and correct the errors in these sentences: "She must went home early." / "He can't have see the message." / "They might have been not ready." / "You must not have told her yet." Then write the correct version and explain why each one was wrong.
40 minFCE Listening — Full practice set
Do a complete FCE Listening test (Parts 1–4). Time yourself: the real test takes about 40 minutes including transfer time. After finishing, go back to any question you got wrong and listen again — identify the exact moment where the correct answer appears.
🔗 Cambridge Listening 🔗 Exam English — Full set
Fri 24/07 Modals — Full review
GrammarUse of English
20 minGrammar: retrieval practice
Without notes: write the 6 modal structures from Monday from memory. Then write one original sentence for each. Check only after you've finished.
40 minFCE Use of English — Full Parts 1–4
Do all four parts in one sitting, timed. The real test allows 75 minutes for Reading + Use of English together. Use 35 minutes for all 4 Use of English parts. After correcting, categorise your errors: vocabulary? grammar? word form?
🔗 Flo-Joe — Full Use of English
End of week 3: Three weeks in, three grammar areas covered. These three — narrative tenses, conditionals, modals — cover a huge proportion of what FCE tests in Use of English and Writing.

Week 4 · Grammar focus
Passive Voice & Reported Speech
27 – 31 July
Mon 27/07 Passive & Reported Speech — Input
GrammarReading
25 minGrammar: passive voice
The passive appears constantly in FCE Use of English Part 4. Focus on: present passive (is made), past passive (was built), and perfect passive (has been discovered). Write 3 sentences about music using the passive: "This song was written by…" / "The album has been listened to by millions…"
🔗 British Council — Passive Voice
35 minFCE Reading — Full Part 5 timed
Do one full Part 5 text timed: 18 minutes maximum. After correcting, go back to the questions you got wrong and find the exact sentence(s) in the text that prove the correct answer.
🔗 Flo-Joe — Reading Part 5
Tue 28/07 Reported Speech — Input & Practice
GrammarWriting
25 minGrammar: reported speech
Focus on tense backshift: "I love this song" → She said she loved that song. Practise with 5 sentences. Use things people in your life actually said this week — it makes it much easier to remember.
🔗 British Council — Reported Speech
35 minFCE Writing — Essay + self-assessment
Write a final essay (140–190 words): "Some people think that faith and science cannot coexist. Do you agree?" After writing, score yourself on 4 criteria (1–5): Content / Organisation / Language range / Accuracy. Be honest.
Personal challenge: This essay topic connects science and faith — two things you actually think about. Write something you actually believe, not the "safe" version.
Wed 29/07 Passive + Reported — Use of English
GrammarUse of English
20 minGrammar: Part 4 transformation practice
Practise the most common FCE Part 4 transformation types using passive and reported speech. Key words to know: SAID, TOLD, REPORTED, KNOWN, BELIEVED, SUPPOSED TO. Example: "People believe he is innocent." → KEY WORD: BELIEVED → "He is believed to be innocent." Find 5 more examples online and practise them.
🔗 Flo-Joe — Part 4 transformations
40 minFCE Use of English — timed Parts 3 & 4
Do Part 3 (10 min) and Part 4 (15 min) under timed conditions. These two parts are where passive and reported speech transformations appear most. After correcting, write a note next to each error explaining what you should have known.
🔗 Exam English — Part 4
Thu 30/07 Month review — Speaking
SpeakingGrammar
20 minGrammar: month recap
From memory, write one example sentence for each of the 4 grammar themes of the month: Narrative Tenses / Conditionals 2 & 3 / Modals for speculation / Passive voice. Don't look back at your notes until after.
40 minFCE Speaking — Full mock (Parts 1–4)
Record a full speaking simulation.

Part 1 (2 min): Answer these: "Tell me about something you enjoyed doing this month." / "What kind of music do you usually listen to and why?" / "What are your plans for the coming months?"

Part 2 (4 min): Compare two photos from Google Images: search "people learning something new" — describe, compare, and speculate for 1 minute each.

Part 3 (3 min): Discuss out loud: What makes a good friend? — go through 5 qualities (honesty / humour / loyalty / support / shared interests) and decide which 2 are most important.

Part 4 (3 min): Answer: "Do you think it's easier to make friends now than it was for previous generations? Why/why not?"
Slow down: You tend to accelerate when speaking. Before each Part, take one breath and start at 80% of your natural speed. It sounds more confident, not less.
Fri 31/07 Final day — Listen back & celebrate
SpeakingListening
30 minListen to your Day 4 & Day 19 recordings
Put them side by side. Listen for: tense consistency, he/she/it, speed, vocabulary range, how you handle pauses. You are not looking for perfection — you are looking for the distance between where you started and where you are now. Write down 3 things that improved.
30 minFree choice
Do whatever part of FCE you enjoyed most this month, or the part you feel least confident in. Your call. You've done 20 days — you've earned the choice.
Well done. 20 days, 4 grammar areas, all 5 FCE skills. That's real preparation — not just reading about the exam, but actually doing it. The classes in August will feel completely different because of this month.
